The Law of Large Numbers

The **Law of Large Numbers** is a fundamental theorem in statistics. Simply put, it states that if you repeat an experiment (like flipping a coin) a very large number of times, the average of the results you get will get closer and closer to the expected value (the theoretical probability).

For a fair coin toss, the theoretical probability of getting "1" (Heads) is $0.5$ (or $50\%$). This doesn't mean that if you flip the coin 10 times, you'll get exactly 5 heads. You might get 3, or 7. But if you flip it **1,000,000 times**, it's almost guaranteed that the number of heads will be very, very close to 500,000, and the observed frequency ($\text{Heads} / \text{Total Tosses}$) will be extremely close to $0.5$.

Conclusion

This simulation visually demonstrates why statistics is so powerful. Even though individual events (a coin toss) are unpredictable, the aggregate behavior of **many** events (thousands of tosses) becomes extremely predictable. Short-term randomness turns into long-term stability.

This is the same principle used in **cybersecurity** to distinguish normal network traffic (millions of packets following a pattern) from an anomaly (an attack deviating from that pattern).
