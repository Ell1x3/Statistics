1. The Bernoulli Approximation of a Counting Process

A Counting Process $\{N(t), t \ge 0\}$ tracks the total number of events that have occurred up to time $t$. The standard model for events occurring randomly, independently, and at a constant rate is the Poisson Process.

We can approximate this continuous-time process by discretizing time. We divide the interval $[0, T]$ into $n$ very small subintervals of length $\Delta t = T/n$. In each subinterval, we perform a Bernoulli Trial:

  • An event occurs with probability $p = \lambda \Delta t = \frac{\lambda T}{n}$.
  • No event occurs with probability $1 - p$.

As $n \to \infty$, the probability of two events happening in the same subinterval vanishes, and the sum of these Bernoulli trials converges to the Poisson distribution.

3. Analysis: Identifying the Stochastic Process

What process does this approximate?

The simulation constructed by dividing time into $n$ subintervals with probability $p = \lambda/n$ approximates a Homogeneous Poisson Process with intensity $\lambda$.

This is a direct application of the Poisson Limit Theorem, which states that the Binomial distribution $B(n, p)$ converges to the Poisson distribution $\text{Pois}(\lambda T)$ when $n \to \infty$ and $p \to 0$ such that $np = \lambda T$ remains constant.

Theoretical Properties

  • Independent Increments: The number of events in disjoint time intervals are independent. The simulation ensures this because each subinterval uses a fresh random number.
  • Stationary Increments: The probability of $k$ events occurring in an interval depends only on the length of the interval, not on *when* it starts. This is reflected in our constant $p$.
  • Rare Events: As $n$ becomes very large, the probability of more than one event occurring in a subinterval becomes negligible ($o(\Delta t)$).

Interpretation of $\lambda$

The parameter $\lambda$ (Lambda) represents the Rate or Intensity of the process. It signifies the **average number of events per unit of time**.

For example, if we simulate cybersecurity breaches with $\lambda = 5$ and $T = 1$ week, we expect, on average, 5 breaches per week. The actual number observed in a simulation ($N(T)$) will fluctuate around this mean with a variance also equal to $\lambda T$.
