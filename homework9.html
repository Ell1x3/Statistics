1. Philosophy of Probability: Interpretations and Axiomatic Resolution

The concept of probability has historically been subject to various philosophical interpretations, each defining what probability means. The Axiomatic Approach, formalized by Andrey Kolmogorov in 1933, resolves the conceptual conflicts by focusing on how probability behaves, providing a unified mathematical framework for all interpretations.

Review of Main Interpretations

Interpretation Definition of P(A) Limitation
Classical (Laplace) Ratio of favorable outcomes to total equally likely outcomes. Requires equiprobable outcomes (e.g., cannot be applied to weather).
Frequentist (Von Mises) Limit of relative frequency as the number of trials ($n$) approaches infinity. Cannot apply to single, non-repeatable events (e.g., a specific cyber attack).
Bayesian (Subjective) Degree of belief based on prior knowledge and new evidence. Subjectivity in assigning prior probabilities.
Geometric Ratio of measure (length, area, volume) of the favorable region to the total space. Limited to continuous spaces with finite measure.

Resolution through the Axiomatic Approach

The Axiomatic Approach bypasses these philosophical debates. It does not define what probability is, but rather establishes three fundamental rules (Kolmogorov's Axioms) that any valid measure of uncertainty must follow.

  • Mathematical Rigor: It shifts probability from a descriptive concept (like the Frequentist view) to a precise mathematical object defined on a measure space.
  • Universal Consistency: By obeying the axioms, results derived from one interpretation (e.g., classical) are guaranteed to be mathematically consistent with results from any other interpretation (e.g., frequentist).

In essence, Kolmogorov's framework provides the "syntax" for probability, allowing all "semantics" (interpretations) to operate within the same rigorous mathematical domain.

2. Probability Theory and Measure Theory

Modern probability theory is fundamentally based on Measure Theory. This connection formalizes the concepts of outcomes, events, and their probabilities using a triple $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$, known as a Probability Space.

The Probability Space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$

  1. Sample Space ($\Omega$): The set of all possible outcomes of an experiment (e.g., all possible network states, all integers from 1 to 6 on a die).
  2. $\sigma$-Algebra ($\mathcal{F}$, Event Space): A collection of subsets of $\Omega$ that represents the **measurable events**. A $\sigma$-algebra is a structure that allows us to perform set operations (union, intersection, complement) and calculate probability measures.
    • It must contain $\Omega$.
    • It must be closed under complementation.
    • It must be closed under countable unions (crucial for connecting discrete and continuous spaces).
  3. Probability Measure ($P$): A function $P: \mathcal{F} \to [0, 1]$ that assigns a probability value (a "measure") to every event in $\mathcal{F}$, satisfying Kolmogorov's Axioms.

Measurable Functions and Random Variables

The concept of a Random Variable ($X$) is formalized as a measurable function from the probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ to a measurable space $(\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B})$, where $\mathcal{B}$ is the Borel $\sigma$-algebra on the real numbers.

$$X: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$$

The function $X$ must be "measurable," meaning that the inverse image of any event in the target space $\mathbb{R}$ must be an event in the original sample space $\Omega$. This is expressed formally as:

$$X^{-1}(B) = \{ \omega \in \Omega \mid X(\omega) \in B \} \in \mathcal{F}, \quad \text{for all } B \in \mathcal{B}$$

This relationship confirms that Probability Theory is simply the study of measures of total size one (since $P(\Omega) = 1$) on a measure space.

3. Derivations from Kolmogorov's Axioms

The three Axioms of Probability (Kolmogorov) are the foundation of all probabilistic theorems:

  1. Axiom 1 (Non-negativity): For any event $A \in \mathcal{F}$, $P(A) \geq 0$.
  2. Axiom 2 (Normalization): The probability of the sample space is one: $P(\Omega) = 1$.
  3. Axiom 3 (Countable Additivity): If $A_1, A_2, \dots$ is a sequence of disjoint (mutually exclusive) events in $\mathcal{F}$, then $P(\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i) = \sum_{i=1}^\infty P(A_i)$.

Derivation A: Subadditivity Property

The Subadditivity Property states that for any countable collection of events $A_1, A_2, \dots$, the probability of their union is less than or equal to the sum of their individual probabilities. This is a powerful generalization of the additivity axiom.

$$P\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i\right) \leq \sum_{i=1}^\infty P(A_i)$$

Proof Outline (using disjointification):

  1. Define a new sequence of disjoint sets $B_i$ such that $\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i = \bigcup_{i=1}^\infty B_i$. The first disjoint set is $B_1 = A_1$. The subsequent disjoint sets are defined recursively: $B_i = A_i \setminus \bigcup_{j=1}^{i-1} A_j$ (the part of $A_i$ not covered by previous $A_j$).
  2. Since $B_i \subseteq A_i$, by the Monotonicity Property (which is a derived consequence of the axioms), we must have $P(B_i) \leq P(A_i)$.
  3. Apply Axiom 3 (Countable Additivity) to the disjoint sequence $B_i$: $$P\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i\right) = P\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty B_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^\infty P(B_i)$$
  4. Substitute the inequality from step 2: $$\sum_{i=1}^\infty P(B_i) \leq \sum_{i=1}^\infty P(A_i)$$
  5. Therefore, $P\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i\right) \leq \sum_{i=1}^\infty P(A_i)$.

Derivation B: Inclusion–Exclusion Principle (for two events)

The Inclusion–Exclusion Principle for two events ($A$ and $B$) is derived by decomposing the union $A \cup B$ into disjoint components.

$$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$$

Proof Outline:

  1. Decompose the union $A \cup B$ into three disjoint sets: $$A \cup B = (A \setminus B) \cup (B \setminus A) \cup (A \cap B)$$ Where $A \setminus B = A \cap B^c$ and $B \setminus A = B \cap A^c$.
  2. Apply Axiom 3 (Additivity) to these disjoint sets: $$P(A \cup B) = P(A \setminus B) + P(B \setminus A) + P(A \cap B)$$
  3. Note that $A$ itself can be decomposed into two disjoint sets: $A = (A \setminus B) \cup (A \cap B)$. Thus, $P(A) = P(A \setminus B) + P(A \cap B)$, implying $P(A \setminus B) = P(A) - P(A \cap B)$.
  4. Similarly for $B$: $P(B \setminus A) = P(B) - P(A \cap B)$.
  5. Substitute $P(A \setminus B)$ and $P(B \setminus A)$ from steps 3 and 4 back into the equation from step 2: $$P(A \cup B) = [P(A) - P(A \cap B)] + [P(B) - P(A \cap B)] + P(A \cap B)$$
  6. Simplify by cancelling one pair of $P(A \cap B)$: $$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$$
