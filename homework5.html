Measures of Central Tendency: Mean, Median, and Mode

Measures of central tendency summarize the position of a statistical distribution by replacing the collected data with a single real value that effectively represents the phenomenon in its globality and summarizes its most important aspects. It is a significant index of the dataset that expresses the position around which the population clusters. Essentially, these measures allow for a description of the order of magnitude of a given phenomenon and its comparison with others.

üîç Observing Reality

Let's imagine we want to compare the performance of students from three classes after a test.

The mere act of collecting the grades of each student in a table does not allow for immediate analysis: it would be more convenient to have a single datum that represents the class's performance.

A value of this type is called a measure of central tendency (or measure of position), and in Statistics, the most important measures of central tendency are precisely the mean, mode, and median.

We can say that the mean, mode, and median are measures of position that allow, based on the requirements, for a synthetic expression of the results of a statistical investigation.

Specifically, the mean, mode, and median are three characteristics of any dataset:

  • The mean is the ratio between the sum of the numerical data and the number of data points;
  • The median is the central value among the numerical data;
  • The mode is the value that appears with the highest frequency among the data.

üß† The Mean ($\bar{x}$) - The Arithmetic Average

The Mean, or arithmetic average, is the most common measure of central tendency. It is calculated by summing all the values in a dataset and then dividing by the total number of values. It utilizes every value in the distribution.

Formula:

The formula for the sample mean ($\bar{x}$) is the sum of all scores ($x_i$) divided by the number of scores ($n$):

$\bar{x} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i}{n}$

Analysis of Class Scores:

5A8, 6, 9, 7, 87.6
5B2, 10, 6, 10, 26.0
5C7, 7, 7, 7, 77.0

Strengths and Weaknesses:

  • Strength: It is mathematically rigorous and takes into account the value of every single observation, making it the best representation of the dataset's center when data are symmetrical.
  • Weakness (Robustness): The Mean is highly susceptible to extreme values (outliers). Notice in Class 5B, the low scores (2, 2) and high scores (10, 10) balance out, giving a misleading Mean of 6.0, even though no student achieved that score. Thus, the mean is not robust to skewed or polarized data.

üîÆ The Median ($Me$) - The Central Value

The Median is the midpoint value of a dataset when the data is arranged in ascending or descending order. It divides the distribution into two equal halves (50% above and 50% below).

Formula:

To find the median, first order the $n$ scores. The median position is found using:

$\text{Position} = \frac{n+1}{2}$

  • If $n$ is odd, $Me$ is the value at the calculated position.
  • If $n$ is even, $Me$ is the average of the two central values (at positions $n/2$ and $(n/2)+1$).

Strengths and Weaknesses:

  • Strength (Robustness): The Median is robust to outliers. If the highest score in Class 5B was 100 instead of 10, the Median would still be 6. It is the best measure for skewed distributions.
  • Weakness: It only considers the position of the values, ignoring the magnitude of the scores, which can discard potentially important information about the tails of the distribution.

‚ú® The Mode ($Mo$) - The Most Frequent Value

The Mode is the value that appears most often in a dataset, i.e., the value with the highest frequency. A distribution can have one mode (unimodal), two modes (bimodal), or no mode at all.

Formula:

There is no computational formula for the Mode; it is found by simple observation (counting frequencies).

  • Strength: It is the only measure applicable to qualitative/nominal data (non-numeric, e.g., favorite color). In Class 5B, it correctly highlights the polarization between low and high scores.
  • Weakness: The Mode may not exist, or a distribution can have multiple modes (as seen in **Class 5B**), making it less useful for uniquely defining the center. It is the least stable measure.

üåê Measures of Dispersion: Variance, Standard Deviation, IQR

Measures of central tendency tell us where the center of the data lies, but they tell us nothing about how spread out the data points are. Measures of dispersion (or variability) quantify the extent to which a distribution is stretched or squeezed.

Understanding dispersion is crucial, especially in fields like cybersecurity, where a wide spread in "risk scores" (like in Class 5B) suggests high uncertainty or heterogeneity, even if the average score is moderate. The main measures of dispersion are the **Variance**, the **Standard Deviation**, and the **Interquartile Range (IQR)**.

We will now analyze how these measures help differentiate the performance of Classes 5A, 5B, and 5C, which had very similar central tendency measures but visibly different data spreads.

üî• The Variance ($\sigma^2$) - Squared Deviation

The Variance measures the average squared difference between each score and the mean ($\bar{x}$). It gives high weight to extreme deviations, indicating how far, on average, the scores are spread from the center.

Formula:

The formula for the population variance ($\sigma^2$) is:

$\sigma^2 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{n}$

Analysis of Class Scores:

Class Mean ($\bar{x}$) Variance ($\sigma^2$) Interpretation
5A7.61.04Low dispersion. Scores are close to the mean.
5B6.012.8Extremely high dispersion, confirming the polarization.
5C7.00.0No dispersion (all scores are identical to the mean).

Strengths and Weaknesses:

  • Strength: It provides a clear, quantitative measure of variability and is foundational for many inferential statistics. It amplifies the effect of large deviations.
  • Weakness: The result ($\sigma^2$) is expressed in units squared, making it difficult to interpret in the original context (1-10 scores). Like the mean, it is highly sensitive to outliers and not robust.

‚ö° The Standard Deviation ($\sigma$) - Interpretable Dispersion

The Standard Deviation is simply the square root of the Variance. This crucial step brings the measure of dispersion back into the original unit of measurement of the scores, making it directly interpretable in the context of the data (scores from 1 to 10).

Formula:

The formula for the population standard deviation ($\sigma$) is:

$\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{n}} = \sqrt{\sigma^2}$

Analysis of Class Scores:

Class Mean ($\bar{x}$) Standard Deviation ($\sigma$) Interpretation
5A7.61.02Scores deviate by ~1 point from the mean (7.6).
5B6.03.58Scores deviate widely (by ~3.6 points) from the mean (6.0).
5C7.00.00Zero dispersion (perfect consistency).

Strengths and Weaknesses:

  • Strength: It provides a clear, interpretable measure of the average spread in the original units. It is the gold standard for normally distributed data.
  • Weakness: Since it is derived from the Mean, the Standard Deviation is not robust; it is heavily influenced by outliers, which can inflate the value of $\sigma$.

üì¶ The Interquartile Range ($IQR$) - Robust Dispersion

The Interquartile Range (IQR) is the difference between the Third Quartile ($Q_3$) and the First Quartile ($Q_1$). It measures the spread of the central 50% of the data, ignoring the most extreme scores (the lowest 25% and the highest 25%).

Formula:

The formula for the IQR is:

$IQR = Q_3 - Q_1$

Analysis of Class Scores:

Class $Q_1$ / $Q_3$ IQR ($Q_3 - Q_1$) Interpretation
5A6.5 / 8.52.0The central 50% of scores span 2 points.
5B2 / 108.0High spread, confirming the central data is also far apart.
5C7 / 70.0Zero spread (perfect consistency).

Strengths and Weaknesses:

  • Strength (Robustness): The IQR is highly robust as it is based on the median and ignores the extreme 25% tails of the distribution. It is the best measure of dispersion for skewed data or data with significant outliers.
  • Weakness: It ignores the most extreme scores, potentially missing important information if those outliers are meaningful (e.g., maximum possible system vulnerability).
